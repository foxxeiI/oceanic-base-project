What is ocean acidification?

Ocean acidification is when CO2 is dissolved into seawater from the atmosphere. Since the Industrial Revolution, humans have burnt fossil fuels (coal, oil, and gas) and released about 1.5 trillion tons of CO2 into the atmosphere. About 30% of that goes into the ocean. When CO2 dissolves, chemical reactions begin that form H+ ions, making the seawater more acidic.

This doesn't mean it's becoming extremely acidic (less than pH 7). It is just becoming less basic. The ocean had a pH of about 8.2 prior to industrialization, is currently at 8.05, and is predicted to decrease to 7.8 by 2100. While this seems small, the chemical reactions happening under the surface of the ocean are massive.

The chemistry behind it

How CO2 makes acid:

CO2(aq) + H2O becomes H2CO3
H2CO3 becomes H+ + HCO3-
HCO3- becomes H+ + CO32-

Carbonic acid is a weak acid; it is not like H2SO4 or HCl which completely dissociate into H+ ions. Some of it remains a molecule of H2CO3 and some will become H+ and carbonate ions. This is an equilibrium reaction. If more CO2 is added to the atmosphere, the equilibrium shifts to the right and creates more H+ ions.

Why shells dissolve: Animals (like oysters, pteropods, and corals) form shells/skeletons out of CaCO3 and need CO32- ions in the seawater to do so. When CO2 is introduced into the seawater, H+ will attach itself to and essentially steal the CO32- ions so it becomes HCO3-, leaving not enough CO32- available for the animals to build and maintain their shells. As more H+ ions join the solution, they lower the pH and make it difficult for shell builders to form their shells.

Saturation state (Omega): Scientists measure whether shell-building is possible in seawater by calculating the saturation state (Omega). The saturation state is defined mathematically as: Omega = [Ca2+][CO32-] / K'sp. Where [Ca2+] is calcium ion concentration, [CO32-] is carbonate ion concentration, and K'sp is the solubility product for aragonite (a form of calcium carbonate). When Omega is greater than 1, the solution is supersaturated with respect to aragonite, meaning shell formation is thermodynamically favorable and calcifying organisms can readily build their skeletons. When Omega drops below 1, the solution becomes undersaturated, and shells dissolve faster than they can be built. As CO2 dissolves into seawater, it consumes carbonate ions, driving down [CO32-] and therefore reducing Omega. The surface ocean Omega is around 3.4 currently, providing ample saturation for shell-builders. However, it is predicted to decrease to 1.5 in 2100 in many regions, crossing below the critical dissolution threshold. In high-latitude regions and upwelling zones, Omega is already approaching or falling below 1, making these areas early warning zones for ocean acidification impacts.

Real-World Impacts

Oyster hatcheries: In the USA on the West coast, oyster hatcheries produce millions of larvae a year. In 2007 and 2008 the oyster hatcheries had massive larval die-offs. The cause was upwelling of water from deeper, lower-pH water, into which the larvae could not form their shells. This lost money (tens of millions of dollars) but the development of IOOS (Integrated Ocean Observing System), a website and reporting system for real-time pH levels, may offer from 1-2 days of advance warning, allowing hatcheries to change their intake water, feeding habits, or other parameters to allow larval development.

Pteropods and Food Webs: Pteropods are tiny floating snails. Fish, whales and seabirds feed on these creatures. Laboratory studies have found that pteropod shells begin to dissolve below a certain pH. When pteropods lose their shells their entire food web is endangered. Shells from pteropods in the Southern ocean already show signs of erosion.

Connection to Coral Bleaching: Corals face both warmer waters and acidification. Studies on 228 different instances show corals experienced 22% loss of calcification and 47% loss of settlement in acid conditions. Recruitment is prevented even when the adult corals survive warm waters.

What Can be Done? Solutions and mitigation

1. Reduce CO2 Emissions: The biggest and best solution is to simply lower our emissions. This is mainly through switching from fossil fuels to solar power, wind power, and so on. The chemistry is simple, the less CO2 entering the atmosphere, the less goes into the ocean and the less acidifying it becomes. Agreements such as the Paris Climate Agreement are seeking to prevent global warming up to 1.5-2 degrees Celsius by cutting global emissions about 50% by 2030 and net-zero by 2050.

2. Protecting Marine Ecosystems: Marine protected areas and marine reserves help ecosystems to regenerate, and in the case of reefs and seagrass, buffer ocean acidification. Taking fishing and trawling out of an area of the ocean can provide a space for animals and marine life to regenerate and build natural buffers.

3. Coastal Adaptation and Monitoring: IOOS data monitoring helps shellfish hatcheries to prepare for and cope with the changes to the ocean environment. They have adjusted their practices and breeding standards for oyster larvae, and may even select breeds of oysters and mussels that can survive in lower pH. Other research efforts involve looking into acid-resistant breeding for other species.

4. Ocean Alkalinity Enhancement: The science behind this is relatively new and it involves increasing the oceans pH. Scientists are experimenting with different substances that could potentially be added to the ocean to achieve this but there are challenges such as high cost, logistical difficulties, and unknown side effects on the wider marine ecosystem.
